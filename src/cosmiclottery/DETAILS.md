# The Cosmic Lottery: Interaction Probability

This simulation explores a conceptual question: *What happens when a probability wave encounters an obstacle?* It demonstrates that interaction and collapse are not guaranteed events, but rather a "Lottery" dictated by quantum probability.

## How Probability is Involved

In this environment, we have a central star, an intermediate planet (at 0.5 AU), and an outer Dyson Sphere detector (at 1.0 AU).

### 1. The Intermediate Interaction
When an expanding probability wave reaches the orbit of the planet, the universe must decide if an interaction occurs. This is the "Cosmic Lottery."
- **The Roll:** The simulation generates a random number between 0 and 1.
- **The Odds:** The planet has a "probability cross-section" of **15%**.

### 2. Scenario A: The Wave Collapses (15% chance)
If the random roll is less than 0.15, the **entire wave instantly collapses** onto the planet.
- The wave is removed from the system.
- A permanent particle mark is left on the planet's surface.
- The photon never reaches the outer Dyson Sphere because it was "detected" (absorbed) early.

### 3. Scenario B: The Wave Passes (85% chance)
If the roll is greater than 0.15, the wave simply ignores the planet. 
- It continues expanding as a non-local probability wave.
- It eventually reaches the outer shell, where it will undergo a second collapse at a random coordinate on the Dyson Sphere.

## Theoretical Significance
This simulation illustrates that the "path" of a photon is actually a series of probabilistic filters. An object in the way of a probability wave doesn't necessarily block it; it merely provides an *opportunity* for collapse. If the lottery is "lost," the wave continues on as if the object never existed, existing in a superposition of paths until a final measurement is forced by the outer shell.

## Visualization Features
- **Detector UI:** The "Cosmic Lottery Detector" in the top-right corner shows the live "dice roll" for each wave as it passes the planet.
- **Dual Counters:** Separate counters track how many photons "lost the lottery" (hit the planet) versus how many "won" and made it to the outer sphere.
