# Quantum Wave Collapse - Dyson Sphere

This 3D simulation explores the exact moment of wavefunction collapse. It visualizes how a 3D probability wave propagates from a star before collapsing into a single particle upon interacting with a massive detector shell.

## How Probability is Involved

In this model, the "photon" does not travel as a little ball. Instead, it exists as a "Probability Wave" that grows larger over time.

### 1. The Expanding Probability Wave
When a photon is emitted from the star, it manifests as a spherical wave. This wave represents the probability of finding the photon at any given point in 3D space. As the sphere expands, the photon is literally "everywhere" on that surface simultaneously.

### 2. The Moment of Interaction (Detection)
The Dyson Sphere shell acts as a massive 3D detector. The simulation monitors the radius of the expanding wave. The exact millisecond the wave's radius equals the shell's radius ($R = 100$), an interaction occurs.

### 3. Wavefunction Collapse
At the moment of interaction, the following happens instantly:
- **Zeroing Out:** The expansive probability wave disappears entirely.
- **Localization:** A single coordinate $(x, y, z)$ on the shell's surface is chosen.
- **Instantiation:** A permanent particle "hit" (a flash and a dot) is rendered at that coordinate.

## Theoretical Significance: Non-Locality
This simulation illustrates **Non-Locality**. When the wave "touches" one part of the shell, the probability of the photon being anywhere else on that 1-AU-wide sphere must instantly drop to zero. The "collapse" is a global state update that happens faster than the speed of light, showing that the underlying quantum state is a single, unified entity rather than a collection of separate parts.

## Controls & Visualization
- **Emission Rate:** Controls how many probability waves are "inflating" in the system at once.
- **Cinematic Rotation:** The shell and the accumulated hits rotate together, emphasizing that the "collapsed" particles are now physical records pinned to a coordinate system.
